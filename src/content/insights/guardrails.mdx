Familiar's comic-vignette engine turns a single told moment into a shareable
animated comic. The obvious way to build that is to hand the whole frame to a
model and hope the style holds. It doesn't. Twelve frames in, the character's
face has drifted, the linework has smoothed out, and the thing that made it feel
authored is gone.

So the constraint layer came first, before any generation. Art direction is a
first-class input: the palette, the line weight, the way a face is allowed to
deform. The AI animates freely, but only inside that box.

<PullQuote>
Guardrails, not gates. A gate stops you. A guardrail keeps you on the road while
you drive fast.
</PullQuote>

This is the same instinct I brought to platform work at scale. You don't make a
system safe by forbidding the interesting cases. You make the safe path the easy
one, then let people move quickly inside it. The style stays human-defined
because the human defined the *space*, not because the human approved every
frame.

<Insight sells="constraint design">
The interesting problem was never the model's raw capability. It was designing
the box: which parts of the art direction are load-bearing and must be locked,
and which can be handed to the machine to improvise inside. That's a systems call,
and it's the difference between a tool that feels authored and one that feels
generated.
</Insight>

The episode this build produced wears three hats: a Familiar feature, the
centerpiece demo on the Workbench, and this writing. One build, told from three
angles. That's the point of leaving the working notes in.
