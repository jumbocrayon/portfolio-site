Open with the **big idea**, stated plainly, in one or two sentences. Not a
warm-up — the provocative claim itself, up front, so a skimmer gets the argument
in the first breath. Everything below is evidence for this line.

Then a paragraph of context: why this question, why now, what changed that makes
it worth an essay. Keep the surface light and inviting; the depth is reached by
the reader's own choice as they descend.

<Breakout kind="takeaway">
The single sentence you'd want a reader to leave with, even if they read nothing
else. Use `kind="takeaway"` for the one-thing-if-you-take-nothing-else box.
</Breakout>

## A section heading names a move, not a topic

Sections carry the argument in steps. Start each with a claim, then earn it.
Prose is the default; the block components are for the moments that want to sit
at a different altitude than the flow.

Use a **pull-quote** when a line is doing real load-bearing work — the thesis, or
a sharp beat worth remembering. Sparingly:

<PullQuote cite="optional attribution">
The line that carries the argument, or the one you'd want quoted back to you.
</PullQuote>

When a passage proves something about *how you work*, name it — but let the reader
infer the character from the specific thing, never claim the trait:

<Insight sells="systems judgment">
What this passage demonstrates about the value you bring. Point at the specific
decision or instinct on display. This is the block that sells the work for you,
so it should reference a real thing you did, not an adjective about yourself.
</Insight>

## Land the argument, don't summarize it

Close on the work, or on the sharpest restatement of the big idea — not a wrap-up
that lists what you just said, and never a line naming your own character. Ending
in the specifics reads as confidence.
