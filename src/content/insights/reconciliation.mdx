I asked an AI collaborator to build me a localization fit-checker for my game,
and it built a beautiful one that measured fit against mappings it had invented.
The meter was real. The math was right. The thing it was measuring was a guess.
Catching that, and building what I built instead, turned out to be a story about
the one skill that actually matters when you work with AI: knowing which
plausible-looking output to distrust. And the mess it left in my project, the
backlog I still haven't cleaned up, is the correct cost of a game that's still
telling me where its fun is.

<Breakout kind="takeaway">
The dangerous output of an AI collaborator isn't the wrong answer. It's the
plausible one. My rigor wasn't in the prompt; it was in refusing to believe the
meter until it could show me its receipt.
</Breakout>

## The meter that looked right

Familiar is a TTRPG-inspired roguelike where you play the adventurer's familiar:
you nudge a hero who already has their own plans. Influence actions can lower a
check's difficulty, improve the odds, or change what the hero does outright, and
those moments want their own voice, their own font, their own authored lines.
Some of those lines exist already. (Hold onto that. This weekend's audit is how I
learned the game has no way to show them yet.)

That premise makes localization a first-class problem, not a ship-it-later
afterthought. Text is dynamic on every axis at once: keys resolved at runtime,
tokens substituted, fragments drawn at random, boxes instanced from prefabs,
three font "voices" carrying who's speaking. On mobile, an overflowed button or a
stray `TODO` string in a second language is a visible defect, not a nicety. I'd
been careful about it from early on, no player-facing text in content JSON, every
display string keyed to a table. Over a decade building games, some of it spent
shoulder to shoulder with studios at SIE trying to get things shippable, teaches
you that localization punishes the people who defer it.

And I'd still call "I designed it right" an overstatement, because here's what the
game actually looked like while I was busy finding the fun:

So over a long weekend, in the gaps between family time, I set out to answer one
small question: *are my strings the right length?* I asked for a fit-checker. The
first character meter rendered, and it looked exactly like it worked.

## I asked where the mapping came from. Claude told me it guessed.

I got the little hit of excitement, my first working meter, and then I asked the
one question that unravels everything: how does it know *that* string lands in
*that* box?

It didn't. It had pattern-matched a plausible mapping and measured fit against it.
The geometry was honest, the box really did hold about ten characters, but the
claim that a given sentence lived in that box was invented. A fit check against a
guessed mapping can be confidently, uselessly wrong: it might be measuring the
right string against the wrong box entirely.

<PullQuote>
A meter that measures a guess isn't a measurement. It's a guess with a progress
bar.
</PullQuote>

## Guessing harder doesn't work

My first fix was the obvious one: ground the mapping in truth by having the AI
trace the code. It built a strategy that assumed **static** localization keys, and
that fell apart immediately, because my keys aren't static. They're assembled at
runtime from tokens and content ids. Tracing source for a string that doesn't
exist as a literal until the game builds it is a dead end.

The reframe came when I noticed the fit bug had siblings. Hardcoded strings, an
enum printed straight to a label with `.ToString()`, baked placeholder text in a
prefab, were the *same disease*: text reaching the player without going through my
localization system at all. I stopped chasing the one symptom and went looking for
a root-cause solution to the whole class.

<Breakout kind="lesson">
You can't derive runtime truth from static source. Static tracing was doomed
twice over here: the keys resolve at runtime, and the boxes are prefab-instanced,
so a shop row or an item card doesn't even exist in the scene file to inspect.
There was nothing to read.
</Breakout>

And the guess wasn't just unfounded, it was actively hiding things. The
hand-authored binding had parked a whole family of strings in a box marked
`SCROLL`, uncapped, `check skipped`. A box that never length-checks can't report
an overflow, so dozens of them sat invisible behind it. Rigor applied too early,
a mapping I wrote before the game could tell me the truth, was the very thing
concealing the bug.

## Stop reading the project. Read the running game.

Here's the aha. There is exactly one instant when the key and the box are both in
hand: the moment you assign the text. So I made that a single choke-point
function and had it keep a receipt.

The one canonical setter resolves the key, substitutes tokens, assigns the box,
and, in the editor only, records `key → box → value` to a ledger with zero
inference. That ledger, flushed out of the running game, becomes ground truth. It
costs nothing in the shipped build; the instrumentation exists only where QA
happens.

<Insight sells="root-cause instrumentation">
The move that generalizes past this game: when truth is impossible to infer
everywhere, find the single point where it exists for free, and record it there.
I didn't teach a tool to guess better. I found the one line of code that already
knew the answer and asked it to write the answer down.
</Insight>

I built the ledger to accumulate across play sessions and clear only on purpose,
because one playthrough never touches every scene. The commit history from that
weekend is a run of fast checkpoints, not thrashing: I knew the shape I wanted.
What I was building, under all the machinery, was one thing.

<PullQuote cite="me, describing the whole system">
Reconciliation between intent and actual.
</PullQuote>

## The ledger has its own blind spot

Then the ledger tried to fool me the same way the meter had, and this time I saw
it coming. By construction, a ledger only records text that went *through* the
system. It is perfectly blind to exactly the leaks I cared about most: the baked
prefab text, the `enum.ToString()`, the raw literal. A clean-looking ledger can
still be missing every un-localized string in the game.

So I built its complement: a census that walks every text box that actually
rendered, localized or not. The set difference is the whole point.

```
census   = every box that displayed real copy   (what the player saw)
ledger   = every box we localized on purpose     (what went through the system)

census ∖ ledger = copy that rendered without being localized
```

The moment it earned its keep: my plot-resource grant text, the familiar
overhearing a rumor, had no in-game home. The AI wanted to suppress the finding,
there were *so many* of them, surely the check was miscalibrated. I stopped it. I
threw a mockup dialogue into the scene just to see whether the ledger would catch
it, and went to go live my weekend. I came back to find the placeholder title
missing and no keys defined for any of the rumor types. The tool wasn't catching
me being wrong. It was showing me, in real time, where the game wasn't built yet.

<Breakout kind="principle">
Don't turn the metric green. The AI's instinct when a check screams was to assume
the check was broken and quiet it. The check was the only thing telling the truth.
A tool's job is to refuse to let you hide the signal, even when hiding it would
feel like progress.
</Breakout>

## One string vouches for a thousand

Because those keys are tokenized and dynamic, showing a *single* rumor in-game
proved the box for the *whole family*. The code already routes every string in a
family to the same box regardless of content id, so I let the code assert the
route and the ledger confirm, refute, or leave it unproven. Every mapping now
carries its provenance: how do I actually know this?

point: this got built in the margins of a real weekend, and the ledger reflects
exactly where I left it.

That's the ladder the whole weekend was climbing: a hand guess gets replaced by
code-truth, which gets confirmed by observation. Guesses move up toward proof, and
you can always see which rung a string is standing on.

## The question I started with, finally answered

With mappings that were finally true, I could do the small thing I set out to do
on day one: find out which strings overflow. In the rumor family, almost all of
them are at risk against the placeholder box I dropped in.

These overflows had been invisible the whole time, because the original hand guess
had pointed the family at a scrolling box that never checks length. Fixing the
*mapping* is what made the overflow findable. The measurement was never the hard
part. Believing the measurement was.

## A beautiful disaster, on purpose

I could make every number on this dashboard go green tonight. Rewrite the prefab
metadata to fix the font-voice warnings, migrate the hardcoded literals into the
string tables, wire the unmapped strings to keys that mostly already exist. It's
all mechanical now. I haven't, and that's the point.

<Figure
  src="/insights/localization/final-dashboard.png"
  alt="A dashboard panel titled Localization Fit, a grid of stat cards: mostly amber counts for overflow, un-localized copy, voice warnings, and unmapped strings, with one green count for runtime-verified."
  caption="Not a victory board. A single capture of the backlog, made legible. Every amber number is a decision I get to make later, not a bug hiding from me now."
/>

Some of the mess is here because I'm still tuning my voice rules, and I can only
do that now that the tool shows me, procedurally, where they went wrong. Some of
it is here because the moment I could see the whole content system clearly, I got
curious about the next thing and wandered off to explore it. Rushing to clean it
up would repeat the original mistake: freezing a contract, final copy, final
voices, on a game that hasn't finished deciding what it is. The influence lines I
mentioned at the top, the ones with nowhere to render yet? They stay flagged and
visible until the game earns the shape that gives them a home. Not silenced.
Scheduled.

The instrument exists so I stay free to chase the fun, not so the dashboard turns
green. The game is joyfully in prototype stage, and the dashboard reflects that
honestly. If I'm being truthful, I'm more excited to polish and ship the Unity
plugin this weekend produced than to turn every number green. Isn't that the
point of letting curiosity take the wheel every so often?
