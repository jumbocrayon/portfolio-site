I've been building a game in the margins of my life: a TTRPG-inspired
roguelike coded entirely with genAI in spare hours, around my kids, on a schedule that
actually works for me. <Conviction>That working style, exploration at my own pace in the margins of a life, has been the whole point of the project</Conviction>, and it's 
how this weekend happened too.

Going in, I had a tool I mostly liked: a content dashboard for editing the data for the game's
encounters. Among other features, it counts the characters in every string and did a little red/yellow/green
guess at whether each one would fit its box. This is the story of how I spent a weekend on what 
I thought would be one small thing: replace the guess with the truth.

That gap (a number that doesn't know whether it's green, yellow or red) is how this whole investigation started. Chasing it
turned into a reminder that LLMs can't replace judgement, taste, and out-of-the-box thinking. 

## Why a game about a familiar makes text hard

In Familiar you don't play the hero. You play their familiar, nudging an adventurer who
already has their own plans. You can lower a check's difficulty, improve the odds of success, or
undermine the adventurer's choice to change what they do outright, and each of those moments wants its own voice, 
font, authored line...

This is a personal passion project largely directed by things I've been burned by before from 
my career in gaming. So naturally I built an overengineered content authoring pipeline
*before* the game that consumes it. It made for a great genAI learning project, let me leverage my TTRPG GM knowledge and 
helped me explore the building-blocks of storytelling through inverted control before futzing with a game. That said, by doing this I knowingly functionally wrote myself a blank check 
for potential useless json fields, unnecessary strings and keys with the wrong voice, and half-built ideas that the bill has officially come due for. 
Some of that was going to turn up no matter how careful about agentic workflow hygeine I'd
been. And I *had* been careful: formal rules about player-facing text separate from data definitions, every display
string keyed to a localizable sidecar table, and written voice rules for how I thought a string ought to be used in-game. 
Over a decade building games, much of it spent trying to get things shippable, taught me that in a game like this, localization is one of those problems that punishes the people who defer it.
HARD. On a mobile game especially, an overflowed button or a stray `TODO` string
has nowhere to hide and meaningfully affects how the playable prototype *feels* to someone you hand your phone to at a party. 
Ignore it, and you're stuck with baked text you can never retrieve and no strategy to guide that separation.

So this was never going to be an "I designed it right." moment, more like "I designed it
thoughtfully while learning a new workflow, and it's time learn how to clean it up."

## The meter that looked right

Over the long weekend, in the gaps between family time, I asked AI to build me a real 
length-checker: a fit meter for *are my strings the right length?* I knew from playtesting the answer was largely no. 
The first set of meters rendered, and the amount of red I saw felt like it confirmed my hypothesis. Except it felt far too easy.

## I asked where the mapping came from. Claude told me it guessed.

That hit of excitement, the idea that my pain point was already solved- then I asked: how do you know *that* string lands in
*that* box?

Long story short, it didn't. It had pattern-matched a plausible mapping based on string keys, 
scenes and textbox names, and measured a character fit against it using the properties of the TMP object.
The geometry was honest, the box really did hold about ten characters in that font at that size, but the
claim that a given sentence lived in that box was quietly invented.

<PullQuote>
A meter that measures a guess isn't a measurement. It's a goose chase.
</PullQuote>

## Whack-a-symptom vs pushing for RCA

My first fix was the obvious one: I told it to ground the mapping in truth by having the AI trace the code, inspecting Unity scenes for TMP objects to build a real map with. 
It built a strategy that assumed the game used **static** localization keys, and of course, that fell apart immediately.
Almost all of my keys leverage the fact that each encounter is authored with multiple rounds, multiple possible skill-checks to 
move to the next beat of the story. They're assembled at runtime from tokens, in-game variables, and content ids. But I will say that 
I did get an awesome TMP extension that let's me push all my static UI labels directly from the Unity Editor out of this step, so it wasn't a total loss :) 

This challenged me to articulate the problem's surface area more concretely, and classify all of the invisible 
localization issues I wanted to fix. Hardcoded strings, incorrect voice fonts, an enum printed straight to a label with `.ToString()`, 
baked placeholder text in a prefab, setting `.text=` directly, were the *same problem*: text reaching the player's screen without going through the 
localization system properly. Basically interest on all of the little shortcuts that AI agents might use to build a 
prototype quickly (not to mention a pesky human who likes to manually pivot/prototype while trying to make the game fun). 
I stopped chasing each symptom and went looking for a solution to my need: I need to understand where the gaps are 
so that I can decide whether I need a validator, new copy, or a completely different UX. And in order to do that, I 
needed to know exactly what text boxes shown to the player, and what contents they were displaying.

Here's a number of sneaky strings, caught in the act, each one wrong for different reasons:

<Breakout kind="lesson">
You can't derive runtime truth from static a source. Static tracing was doomed
twice over here: the keys resolve at runtime, and the boxes are prefab-instanced,
so a shop row or an item card doesn't even exist in the scene file to inspect.
There was nothing to read.
</Breakout>

The first few passes were very messy. Following the localization keys to the logical place in-game was a doomed strategy 
because I'd started with a content pipeline rather than a game. Over the course of building my schema and localization strategy, 
I had described the 'Distract' action from both the familiars perspective ("You bark loudly, drawing attention away from {PC_NAME}" and 
"Swooping with a flash of feather, you feel eyes on you as {PC_NAME} acts") - 
separate from using the game explanation voice ("Lowers the DC of {PC_NAME} by 2"). 
In this version of the game, the per-familiar narrative strings were never used, but the explanation text was. 
The strings and the question of their utility had long been forgotten - I needed my dashboard to 
help me see what was and wasn't being used so that I could make the decision of whether the game needed it.

## Put out-of-the-box thinking inside the box

Somewhere in between family activities, my brain caught up with the clear solution. 
There is exactly one instant when the key and the box are both in hand: the moment you assign the text. 
I built a runtime ledger to act as the source of truth for which keys got localized to what boxes with what settings. 

The canonical localization setter resolves the key, substitutes tokens, assigns the box,
and, in the editor only, records `key → box → value` to a ledger with zero
inference. That ledger, built out of the running game, serves as the source of truth for what goes through the 
localization system. It costs nothing in the shipped build; the instrumentation exists only where dev
and QA happen (a fine place to take a performance hit).

<Insight sells="root-cause instrumentation">
The move that generalizes past this game: when truth is impossible to infer
everywhere, define a source of truth you can defend, and record it there.
I didn't teach a tool to guess better. I found the one class that already
knew the answer and promoted it to accountant.
</Insight>

I built the ledger to accumulate across play sessions and clear on demand,
because one playthrough rarely touches every scene. I was building a cohesive strategy to enforce 
"localization as a first-class citizen" without needing to build a fully automated regression test setup
 for a prototype-stage game.

Then I put it where I'd trip over it. The ledger lives in the Unity editor where I playtest, 
and every time I play the game or spot-check a feature, the receipt is right there.

Don't hide the mess. Make the reconciliation between intent and what's actually happening something I can't ignore.

## One accountant wasn't enough for the complete picture

Then the ledger tried to fool me the same way the meter had, but this time I saw
it coming. The ledger only records text that already went *through* the localization 
system. It is totally blind to everything else: baked prefab text, the `enum.ToString()`, the raw literal. 
A clean-looking ledger can still be missing every un-localized string in the game.

So I built another Editor-only complement: a census that records every text box that actually gets 
rendered, localized or not. The reconciliation layer is where the magic happens.

```
census   = every TMP box that displayed real copy   (what the player saw)
ledger   = every TMP box localized in-game     (what went through the system)

census ∖ ledger = copy that rendered without being localized
```

Immediately the census flagged a whole family of strings (plot-resource grants) - 
basically the familiar overhearing a rumor that drives plot -  
that I'd authored but never wired into the flow of the game.
This is another content-pipeline-first project problem: copy written 
ahead of the scene that would show it, moving so fast that I'd genuinely forgotten these 
(load bearing) lines existed. Oops! The game worked without them, but I'd definitely 
felt like the narrative pacing was off for a while. 

The AI wanted to suppress the finding, because there were *so many* of them, surely the
check was miscalibrated. I stopped it. I threw a mockup dialogue into the scene just to 
see whether the ledger would catch it, and went to go live my weekend. I came back to 
find the dialog title flagged as a gap, but the strings verified. The tool was finally 
fulfilling the need I had of genAI native development: 
showing me the gap beween two systems that had been authored independently.

<Breakout kind="principle">
Don't just turn the dashboard green. The AI's instinct when a check screamed was to assume
the check was broken and quiet it, or infer why the rule was wrong (no schema contracts 
for localization tokens was an early choice I'd made to support iteration). The check was 
the only thing telling the truth. A good dashboard cannot hide the signal, even when the signal 
appears overwhelming, like it did to the poor AI agent implementing it.
</Breakout>

## One string vouches for a thousand

Because localization keys are tokenized and dynamic, showing a *single* rumor in-game 
proved the box for the *whole family*. The last piece was teaching the tool how my keys 
are actually assembled: that a key is built at runtime from parts like `influenceAction`, 
`roundNumber`, and `skillCheck`. I built a skill to scan localization files for patterns where 
one observed string could stand in for every sibling that shares its shape, and review the 
code to verify the pattern before adding it to a codepath ledger. 
The same box will always display an overheard rumor, regardless of which rumor 
the player hears, so this was a great example of where to let the code assert the route and 
the reconciliation confirm, refute, or leave that family of keys unproven. 
Every mapping now carries its provenance: how do I actually know this?

That's the ladder the whole weekend was climbing: a naive guess gets replaced by
a better question, which gets confirmed by observation. As I playtest more, I see strings 
move from "codepath confirmed" to "runtime verified" and know exactly why, when and how.

## The question I started with, finally answered

With mappings that were finally true, I could do the small thing I set out to do
on day one: find out which strings overflow. In the rumor family, almost all of
them are at risk against the placeholder box I dropped in.

The measurement was never the hard part: the first thing the AI produced was a quality check for 
whether at a given font-size, a string would fit. Getting the game to a place where that check 
could be used reliably as I prototype and add new boxes all the time, was a different challenge.

## A beautiful disaster, and I'm leaving it that way (for now)

I could make every number on this dashboard go green tonight. Rewrite the prefab
metadata to fix the font-voice warnings, migrate the hardcoded literals into the
string tables, wire the unmapped strings to keys that mostly already exist. It's
mostly mechanical but I haven't, and that's the whole crux of why I chose a dashboard at all. 
This is very much a prototype that needs time to breathe and no amount of AI-assisted 
speed will tell me which of these numbers should be green, and which 
aren't worth chasing at all. And that's kind of the fun part anyway. Building a 
dashboard to set burndown goals is tempting in a lot of ways, but at this stage I 
want to build dashboards to make sure I have the data to support my decisions.

Some of the mess is here because I'm still tuning my voice rules, and I needed help 
looking at which decisions to stand on, which to automate away, and which to think about.
Some of it is here because the moment I could see the whole content system clearly, I got
curious about the next thing and wandered off to explore it. Rushing to clean it
up would repeat the original mistake: freezing a contract, making rules for copy a game
didn't actually support, vague voice rules not documented in a way the agents could adhere to, 
on a game that hasn't finished deciding what it is. The rumor lines the census caught,
the ones with nowhere to render yet? Deleted on purpose, not silenced. In fact, a UX change 
where longer narrative strings appear one sentence at a time was where I chose to go next after 
reviewing the data in its entirety, and turning the dashboard green would have robbed me of the 
opportunity to explore a more visual-novel inspired direction entirely which I'm loving.

Since the weekend in question, I've been able to make a lot of fast decisions. I've completely overhauled the UI/narrative flow, weighed 
the pros and cons of switching engines entirely via AI-driven analysis, and found (and fixed) a handful more 
edge-cases that my reconciliation layer wasn't handling properly. The core strategy has proven itself out, and 
it's something solodev and indies can introduce no matter where they are in the development cycle.
<Conviction>Choosing to make localization a first-class constraint from a projects inception isn't possible for everyone, but a 
path to giving players content in their native language should be.</Conviction>
