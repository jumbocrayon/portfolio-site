# Design Direction — Look, Feel, and Interaction

## The feeling to produce
The "oh, *this* person" moment, in three stacked layers (and the order matters):
1. **Whimsy that drives the work forward** — the hook. Playful, alive, a little handmade. Something you want to poke at.
2. **A feel for partnering with me** — the character read. The values I won't negotiate are visible in *how the site behaves*, not just stated.
3. **Systems-level depth** — the proof. When someone goes deeper, they find real technical substance and analysis, cleanly presented.

A visitor should be able to skim the whimsy in 10 seconds, get the character in a minute, and — if they want — fall all the way through to the depth.

## Visual language
- **Handmade, true-to-me, not corporate.** Hand-drawn marks, imperfect lines, personal warmth. The aesthetic being human-defined (not technically perfect) is *the thesis made visible*. Lean into "I drew this well enough and it has soul" over "a designer polished this."
- **Whimsy with intent.** Motion, playful microinteractions, and surprise — but every playful element should *do something* or *reveal something*, never pure decoration. Whimsy in service of the work.
- **Depth-on-demand.** Surfaces stay light and inviting; depth is reachable but never forced on the casual visitor. Progressive disclosure: a demo's playful surface, then "how it works," then the real systems thinking underneath.

## Interaction principles
- **Demos are touchable, not described.** Wherever possible, let the visitor *do the thing* rather than read about it.
- **The site itself is a demo.** Its own interactions should exemplify the build-in-public, curiosity-first ethos — e.g. a sense that the site is alive and evolving, that experiments are running.
- **Shareable moments.** The centerpiece (and ideally the site) should produce little artifacts people want to share — that's both the product thesis and the distribution mechanism.

## Structural metaphor
A **collection of demos / living testbed**, not a linear narrative or a project grid. Think: a workbench where experiments are laid out, some finished, some in progress, with the build visible. Visitors browse experiments rather than reading a CV.

## Tone of copy
- First person, warm, clever-not-arrogant, compassionate-not-soft.
- Communicate systems thinking in human terms.
- No buzzword soup. Premium is conveyed by the work doing the convincing, never by claiming it.
- **Register is playful-PRECISE, not playful-casual:** whimsy proposes, rigor delivers. See `03-VOICE-GUIDELINES.md` for the full diction do/don't list, the "one quiet anchor" credibility rule, and the 5-question self-check — required reading before writing any visitor-facing copy.

## Anti-goals (what to avoid)
- A traditional case-study grid that reads like a portfolio template.
- Over-polished, agency-corporate slickness that erases the handmade personality.
- Whimsy as empty decoration disconnected from the work.
- Forcing depth on every visitor — keep the surface inviting.
