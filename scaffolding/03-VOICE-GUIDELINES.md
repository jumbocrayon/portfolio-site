# Voice & Tone Guidelines — Jess Loeb Portfolio

> **Status: working draft, far from final.** These rules were derived from a
> first tone pass and will keep shifting as the copy does. **Revisit and reconcile
> this guide after any large copy edit** (a new page, a restructured page, a batch
> of rewrites). When a guideline and a shipped line disagree, that's a signal to
> update the guide, not to silently follow the stale rule.

> Read this before writing or revising any visitor-facing copy on the site
> (page text, hooks, CTAs, demo microcopy, empty states, error states, alt text
> that's read aloud). It governs the *shell* and every demo that slots into it.

## The one-sentence rule
**Whimsy proposes, rigor delivers.** Every playful line should be backed, in the
same breath or the same paragraph, by something precise. Lightness is the top
layer of a deep person, never the whole personality.

## Who's speaking (and what they've earned)
The voice belongs to a Director-level platform engineer: console-launch
architecture at 24M+ players with zero downtime, a U.S. patent in observability,
studios and departments built from zero, years of A/B testing and analytics at
scale. The experimentation here is **disciplined**: hypotheses, instrumentation,
risk taken deliberately *while it's still cheap to learn*, not unstructured
tinkering. Copy should sound like someone whose play is informed by that rigor,
even when the rigor isn't named.

The CV's own register is the north star: *"I lead with vision and curiosity,
stay hands-on where it matters."* / *"values-driven rigor."* / *"complexity
translation: communicate complex systems simply while still diving into
implementation details."* Port that voice; don't invent a new one.

## The register we're aiming for
**Playful-precise**, not playful-casual. The line to walk:
- We do **not** take ourselves too seriously.
- We take the **quality of the work, and the thinking behind it, very seriously.**

If a line is fun but says nothing true about how Jess works, it's decoration:
cut it or back it. If a line is rigorous but joyless, warm it up. The goal is
both at once.

## The three-layer altitude (order matters, it's the site's spine)
1. **Whimsy in service of the work** (the hook): playful, alive, a little
   handmade; every playful element *does* or *reveals* something.
2. **A feel for partnering with Jess** (the character read): values visible in
   *how the copy behaves*, not just stated.
3. **Systems-level depth** (the proof): real substance, reached by choice.

Surface copy stays light and inviting. Depth is **reached by the visitor's own
choice**, never pushed on them and never poured over them.

## Voice attributes (with the failure mode each guards against)
These describe the *target*, for the writer. They are **not vocabulary for the
copy** (see the next section).
- **Warm, not chummy.** Friendly and human; not over-familiar or cutesy.
- **Clever, not arrogant.** Wit that includes the reader; never a flex.
- **Compassionate, not soft.** Kind *and* high-standards. Both, not either.
- **Precise, not corporate.** Specific, plain, concrete. No buzzword soup, and
  no agency-slick filler. Premium is shown by the work, never claimed.
- **Confident, not loud.** One quiet credibility anchor beats a wall of numbers.

## Show the character, never name it (hard rule)
The attributes above are how the copy should *feel*; they are **never words the
copy uses about Jess.** Naming a trait is the surest way to undercut it. Calling
yourself "clever" is the least clever-looking move available; "compassionate"
and "not soft" read as a self-review. The site's whole thesis is *demonstrate,
don't assert*, and that applies hardest to character.

- **Don't** write a summary line that lists who Jess is ("clever without being
  arrogant, compassionate without being soft").
- **Do** let the reader *infer* the trait from a specific thing Jess did, the
  stance a sentence takes, or the way the page behaves. A page that simply
  *behaves* clever and kind needs no line claiming it.
- When tempted to summarize the character at the end of a page, **cut the line
  instead.** Ending on the work, in silence, reads as confidence. (Both the home
  About page and the `/how-i-play` services page do this: the home closes by
  bridging softly to the Workbench, not by naming who Jess is; the services page
  ends on its last engagement card with no wrap-up.)
- The credibility version of this is the "one quiet anchor" rule below: show one
  earned proof, don't recite a résumé.

## Credibility: the "one quiet anchor" rule
Decided for this site: the shell stays whimsy-forward, with **one understated
credibility signal** near the top (e.g. the console-launch nod in a page hook)
so a casual visitor registers "senior operator" without reading a résumé.
Hard credentials (PlayStation, PS5, the patent, scale numbers) live in the
**depth layers**, where the work earns them. **Don't lead a hook with a job
title or a metric** — the hook stays a hook.

**The home (About) page is the exception that proves the rule.** It is the
credibility/story layer, so it carries the anchors — but they are *reached by
scrolling past the story*, never front-loaded into the hook. The hook is still
just name + role line + the `AI enthusiast*` motif; the metric-bearing highlight
cards live below, met by choice. Same principle: numbers are earned by the
visitor's descent, not thrown at the casual reader.

## Signature phrase
**"Curious content."** This is Jess's own phrasing for the work she curates, and
it's the preferred frame over "experiments in public" or anything mad-scientist.
Use it as the role/tagline anchor and reach for it when describing what the site
is about. It carries the whole thesis (applied curiosity + craft) in two words.

## The through-line: applied systems-thinking, curiosity-led imagination

Underneath the whimsy, every page is arguing one philosophy, compressed into the
home page's tagline: **applied systems-thinking, curiosity-led imagination.**
Curiosity picks the direction; disciplined systems-thinking makes it real and
keeps it honest. The two are never at odds — the rigor is what lets the play
mean something.

The value claim this philosophy makes is about **discipline as the premium**: in
a moment when almost anything is easy to build, the scarce thing is building
something imaginative that doesn't oversell what it can actually do. Copy can
lean on this argument when it needs to name the value, because it's a claim about
the *market and the method*, not a claim about Jess's character (which still
follows the show-don't-tell rule). The turn always lands on **empowering the
human doing the creative work**, never on replacing them.

Guardrails:
- **Show the discipline, don't recite it.** Don't stack self-labels ("careful,"
  "disciplined," "rigorous," "thoughtful") — that's the name-the-trait failure
  the show-don't-tell rule guards against. Let the specifics and the work carry
  it; the tagline is the one compressed statement, and it earns its place because
  it's a method, not an adjective about the person.
- **Keep agency on the visitor.** Depth is reached by choice; the home page
  closes on "go as deep as you're curious to." Don't do the frame *to* the
  reader.
- **Curiosity and rigor together, never one without the other.** A playful line
  with no method behind it is decoration; a rigorous line with no curiosity is
  joyless. Same both-at-once balance as compassionate-not-soft.
- **Name specific tools sparingly.** The philosophy is about judgment and craft,
  not any one technology. When a specific tool (AI included) is the subject, it's
  the occasion for the thinking, not the point of it — that framing lives on
  its own `/how-i-play` engagement card, not spread across the shell.

## Punctuation: em-dash discipline
Em dashes (—) were overused in the first pass and read as a tic. **Budget: rare.**
Default to commas, periods, colons, or restructured sentences. Before shipping a
line with an em dash, try a comma or a period first; keep the dash only if the
sentence genuinely needs the harder break. Reserve it for a deliberate structural
separator, never as a default beat.

## Diction — verbs and framing
**Reach for** active verbs the visitor owns, and experiment language that implies
method:
- *go deeper · dig in · open it up · take a closer look · explore · try · give it a turn*
- *test an idea · a hypothesis · in the open · working notes · still being
  figured out · cheap to learn · while the stakes are low*

**Avoid / use sparingly:**
- **"experiments in public" as the headline frame** → reads mad-scientist /
  unstructured. Prefer *"curious content"* or *"test ideas in the open."*
  ("Experiment" as the content *type* is fine; as the *vibe* it overshoots.)
- **"fall as deep as you like" / "fall all the way through"** → "fall" makes
  depth passive and has an unintended come-on undertone. Depth is an **active
  choice the visitor makes**: *"go as deep as you're curious to."*
- **"poke" / "poke me" anywhere** → reads high-school and childlike. **Don't use
  it**, not even once. For the literal try-it toy, use *"try it" / "give it a
  turn."* For looking closer at an experiment, use *"take a closer look."*
- **"wires showing" / "the cheapest demo" / self-deprecation that devalues the
  work** → build-in-public honesty is great; don't undercut the craft to get it.
  Prefer *"working notes left in on purpose."*
- **"ow!", cutesy interjections, exclamation-heavy reactions** → warmth without
  the wink-at-yourself flinch.

## A note on safety as a woman in tech
This is a real constraint, not a stylistic preference. Avoid phrasings that,
out of context to a stranger, could read as flirtation, invitation to
intimacy, or anything that lowers the visitor's sense that this is a
professional's space. "Fall as deep as you like" failed this test. When in
doubt, keep agency on the **visitor's** side ("go as deep as *you're* curious")
rather than on Jess's side ("let me take you down"). Warm ≠ inviting closeness.

## Quick before/after calibration
| ❌ Off (playful-casual) | ✅ On (playful-precise) |
|---|---|
| "I run my own interactive-media experiments in public." | "Curious content, built in the open." |
| "the wires are showing on purpose" | "the working notes left in on purpose" |
| "fall as deep as you like" | "go as deep as you're curious to" |
| "poke this experiment →" | "take a closer look →" |
| "poke me" (the toy) | "try it" / "give it a turn" |
| "you found the cheapest demo on the site." | "this is the simplest demo here, on purpose." |
| "every poke is a tiny experiment." | "every interaction is a tiny hypothesis." |
| em dash used as a default beat | comma, period, or colon (dash kept only when needed) |

## The 5-question self-check (run before shipping any new copy)
1. **Backed?** Is every playful line backed by something true/precise nearby?
2. **Altitude?** Does the surface stay light, with depth reached *by choice*?
3. **Agency?** Is the visitor the one going deeper, not being taken deeper?
4. **Earned?** Are credentials shown by the work, not claimed up front (one
   quiet anchor max in the shell)?
5. **Clean?** No "poke" anywhere, and em dashes kept rare (try a comma first)?

If a line fails any of these, it's not ready. Revise, don't ship.
